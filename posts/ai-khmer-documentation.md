For most of the 20th century, Khmer was an oral language as much as a
written one. Court archives, hospital intake, university lectures,
family histories — all spoken, very little captured.

Modern speech recognition changes the economics. A single hour of audio
that used to cost $80 to transcribe by hand now costs less than a coffee.

## Three institutions, three approaches

In this piece we walk through how three institutions — the
**Royal University of Phnom Penh**, **BBC Khmer**, and a midsize NGO —
are using Smean to turn audio archives into searchable corpora.

### Royal University of Phnom Penh

Every lecture in the economics faculty is now recorded and transcribed.
Students search across an entire semester by topic, not by date.

### BBC Khmer

Twenty years of radio archives are being indexed. Reporters can now
search interview audio for a phrase and jump to the exact second it
was spoken.

### The NGO

Field interviews with community-health workers — historically
discarded after transcription — are being preserved as a training
corpus for a domain-specific health vocabulary.

The common thread: capturing speech used to be the bottleneck. Now
storage and search are trivial; the bottleneck moved to *deciding
what to listen for*.
